Bycatching

Bycatching, in the fishing industry, is the act of unintentionally catching a certain target species or target size of fish or other marine life.

Every day, thousands of miles of nets and lines are set in the ocean to catch fish. Although this is essential for catching seafood we ultimately consume, it can be harmful for the marine environment.

Dolphins, sea turtles, and other marine life often get stuck in these nets and lines. When they are hauled up with the rest of the catch, these marine animals are discarded back into the water. These animals are often dead or dying when they are discarded.

Impacts

Bycatching can have serious consequences. Endangered marine life, especially those already suffering from habitat loss, are at even greater risk for going extinct.

Bycatching not only affects fish, but also endangers populations of whales, dolphins, sea turtles, seabirds, and other precious marine life. Approximately 200,000 loggerhead sea turtles are caught a year, with almost 700 killed in the U.S. alone.

Environmental concerns aside, bycatching is also harmful for fishermen, as it damages their gear, reduces their number of catches.

Solutions

Bycatching is a known problem throughout the industry, and many are taking steps to reduce it. Luckily, new technology allows for fishermen to reduce the number of bycatch, and to catch more of the targeting species and size.

Because of this, fishing is no longer poses a threat to the survival of non-target species. But the new technology is far from perfect, and not employed everywhere.

The continuation of developing new technologies to catch only the desired species and size is essential for solving the problem of bycatching, or at least taking great strides in reducing it.

Take Action

There are steps you, as the consumer, can take to mitigate the effects of bycatching.

Tuna and shrimp are some of the most common types of seafood consumed in the United States. Unfortunately, they also create large environmental impacts, as the amount of bycatch for these two is large. Some fisheries that are the source for tuna and shrimp are more maintainable than others, however.

Check online to see which sources are more environmentally friendly. Try to avoid problematic fisheries, and promote sustainable ones.

Most importantly, get involved and spread the word!
